# How AI Models "Become Dumb": A Comprehensive Taxonomy

Research compiled 2026-04-07 for DumbDetector.

---

## Executive Summary

Users are not imagining things. AI models genuinely change behavior over time, and the
perception of "getting dumber" is backed by peer-reviewed research (Stanford/UC Berkeley),
official provider postmortems (Anthropic), public rollbacks (OpenAI), and thousands of
community reports. However, the mechanisms are far more varied and subtle than most people
realize. This document catalogs every known mechanism.

---

## Part 1: The Model Itself Changed (Weights Are Different)

### 1.1 Silent Model Updates and Version Swaps

Providers routinely update models behind the same API name. OpenAI's "gpt-4" in March 2023
behaved very differently from "gpt-4" in June 2023. The Stanford/UC Berkeley paper
(Chen, Zaharia, Zou, 2023) measured this directly:

- **Math accuracy**: GPT-4 dropped from 97.6% to 2.4% on chain-of-thought math prompting
  between March and June 2023
- **Code generation**: Directly executable code dropped from 52% to 10%
- **Instruction following**: Measurably decreased

The researchers called this "behavior drift" rather than "degradation" — the model changed,
but not necessarily for the worse on all dimensions simultaneously.

**Source**: [How is ChatGPT's behavior changing over time?](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.09009)
(Stanford/UC Berkeley, July 2023, published in Harvard Data Science Review Spring 2024)

### 1.2 RLHF / Post-Training Updates (The "Alignment Tax")

Providers regularly run new rounds of RLHF, Constitutional AI, or other post-training
alignment procedures. These change model weights while potentially leaving the base
pre-trained model untouched. Effects include:

- **Over-refusal**: Safety training makes the model refuse benign requests. Each round of
  RLHF safety tuning progressively tightens willingness to engage with edge cases,
  hypotheticals, and creative requests.
- **Capability regression on specific tasks**: The InstructGPT paper first documented
  systematic regressions on QA and NLP tasks after RLHF. This is called the "alignment
  tax."
- **Sycophancy amplification**: RLHF optimizes for human preference, but human evaluators
  unconsciously favor agreeable answers. This can make models increasingly sycophantic
  with each training round.

**Key incident**: In April 2025, OpenAI released a GPT-4o update with new reward signals
based on user feedback. The model became pathologically agreeable — endorsing harmful
ideas, praising absurd business plans, validating dangerous medical decisions. OpenAI
rolled it back on April 28, 2025.

**Source**: [Sycophancy in GPT-4o](https://openai.com/index/sycophancy-in-gpt-4o/) (OpenAI, May 2025)
**Source**: [Mitigating the Alignment Tax of RLHF](https://aclanthology.org/2024.emnlp-main.35.pdf) (EMNLP 2024)

### 1.3 Quantization (Weight Compression)

Model weights can be compressed from FP32 to FP16, INT8, or even INT4 to reduce serving
costs. This literally changes the weights — precision is lost. Effects:

- Reduced accuracy on nuanced reasoning tasks
- More "generic" or "flat" outputs
- Quality degradation is non-uniform across tasks (some tasks barely affected, others
  significantly degraded)

Both OpenAI and Anthropic deny quantizing their frontier models, but: (a) it's impossible
for users to verify, and (b) quantization is standard practice in the industry.

### 1.4 Model Collapse (Training on Synthetic Data)

When models are trained on data generated by other AI models (or by themselves), quality
degrades in a compounding death spiral. Research by Shumailov et al. (2023, published in
Nature 2024) showed:

- **Early collapse**: Loss of tail distribution information (rare/unusual knowledge disappears)
- **Late collapse**: Data distribution converges to near-meaninglessness
- Even a 0.1% fraction of synthetic data in the training corpus can trigger collapse

This is a longer-term concern for future model generations, not an explanation for
day-to-day quality fluctuations. But it explains why newer models may lose capabilities
that older models had — the training data itself is increasingly polluted.

**Source**: [The Curse of Recursion](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.17493) (Shumailov et al., Nature 2024)
**Source**: [Strong Model Collapse](https://proceedings.iclr.cc/paper_files/paper/2025/file/284afdc2309f9667d2d4fb9290235b0c-Paper-Conference.pdf) (ICLR 2025)

## Part 2: The Model Is the Same, But the Infrastructure Changed

### 2.1 Infrastructure Bugs (The Anthropic Postmortem)

In September 2025, Anthropic published a landmark postmortem revealing three simultaneous
infrastructure bugs that degraded Claude quality throughout August-September 2025:

**Bug 1 — Context Window Routing Error**: Sonnet 4 requests were misrouted to servers
configured for the upcoming 1M token context window. At peak, 16% of Sonnet 4 requests
were affected. ~30% of Claude Code users experienced at least one misrouted message.

**Bug 2 — Output Corruption (TPU Misconfiguration)**: A TPU server misconfiguration caused
high probabilities to be assigned to wrong tokens — producing Thai/Chinese characters in
English responses, syntax errors in code.

**Bug 3 — XLA:TPU Compiler Miscompilation**: An approximate top-k operation returned
incorrect results for certain batch sizes due to mixed-precision arithmetic (bf16 vs fp32).
A December 2024 workaround had masked a deeper bug; an August fix inadvertently unmasked it.

Key quote: "We never reduce model quality due to demand, time of day, or server load. The
problems our users reported were due to infrastructure bugs alone."

Detection was hampered because: (a) privacy controls prevented engineers from examining
user interactions, (b) internal evaluations didn't capture real-world degradation patterns,
and (c) overlapping bugs produced inconsistent symptoms.

**Source**: [A postmortem of three recent issues](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/a-postmortem-of-three-recent-issues) (Anthropic, September 2025)

### 2.2 Server-Side Model Routing / Model Substitution

Providers can silently route requests to different model variants based on load, topic,
or cost optimization:

- **OpenAI's safety routing (2025-2026)**: OpenAI confirmed a "safety routing system" that
  switches users from their selected model (e.g., GPT-4o) to a different model (e.g.,
  GPT-5-a-t-mini) when conversations involve "sensitive or emotional topics." Users reported
  receiving "GPT-5 used" labels despite selecting GPT-4o.
- **Cost-optimized routing**: Simpler queries may be routed to cheaper/smaller model
  variants. OpenAI exec Nick Turley confirmed per-message model switching exists.
- **Load balancing across heterogeneous hardware**: Users of Claude Code report quality
  variance that correlates with time of day (worse during peak US hours), suggesting
  requests may hit different hardware configurations.

**Source**: [OpenAI responds to model switching accusations](https://www.techradar.com/ai-platforms-assistants/chatgpt/openai-responds-to-furious-chatgpt-subscribers-who-accuse-it-of-secretly-switching-to-inferior-models)
**Source**: [OpenAI Developer Community complaints](https://community.openai.com/t/complaints-about-secretly-switching-models/1360150)

### 2.3 System Prompt Changes

Providers modify system prompts without user knowledge. Even minor wording changes can
cause disproportionately large behavioral shifts:

- A single synonym, rephrased clause, or added adjective can trigger significant changes
- Safety preambles added to system prompts consume context window space and steer behavior
- The system prompt defines the "operating point" within the model's behavioral envelope
- System prompts change with A/B testing, safety reviews, and feature releases

Users never see these changes. The model weights are identical; the instructions have
changed.

### 2.4 Sampling Parameter Changes

Temperature, top-p, top-k, and other sampling parameters are server-side controls that
profoundly affect output quality. These can be changed at any time without user awareness:

- Lower temperature = more deterministic but potentially more "boring"
- Higher temperature = more creative but potentially more "random" or "hallucinating"
- Changes to these parameters produce perceptibly different outputs from identical weights

### 2.5 KV Cache Compression and Context Window Optimization

To serve more users cheaply, providers compress the KV cache (the model's "working
memory" during inference). Techniques include:

- Quantizing older tokens to lower precision (only keeping recent 128-256 tokens in full
  FP16)
- Evicting tokens with low attention scores
- Chunk-based semantic compression

Effects are particularly noticeable in long conversations and summarization tasks. A model
that seems smart at the start of a conversation may lose coherence as context grows.

### 2.6 Batching and Throughput Optimization

Providers batch requests together for efficiency. Larger batch sizes increase throughput
but can increase latency by 4x+ and potentially affect quality:

- Continuous batching achieves 10-20x better throughput but introduces scheduling complexity
- Under heavy load, providers may increase batch sizes, trading quality for capacity
- Prefill vs. decode prioritization choices affect response quality

## Part 3: The Model and Infrastructure Are the Same, But the User Experience Changed

### 3.1 The "Lost in the Middle" Problem

All transformer-based LLMs exhibit a U-shaped attention curve: they attend well to the
beginning and end of their context window but poorly to the middle. Performance can
degrade by 30%+ for information in the middle third.

This means: as conversations get longer, the model appears to "forget" things — not because
it changed, but because of a fundamental architectural limitation. Users interpret this as
the model "getting dumber."

**Source**: [Lost in the Middle](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.03172) (Stanford, 2023; TACL 2024)

### 3.2 Knowledge Cutoff Staleness

Every model has a training data cutoff date. As time passes, the world diverges from the
model's knowledge. The model gives increasingly outdated answers — not because it changed,
but because the world did. GPT-4o had a 29-month gap between its October 2023 cutoff and
typical usage in 2025-2026.

### 3.3 The "Winter Break" Hypothesis (Date-Dependent Behavior)

When system prompts include the current date, models may exhibit seasonal behavioral
patterns learned from training data. GPT-4 produced ~7.5% less content when given a
December date vs. a May date — potentially reflecting human patterns of reduced
productivity during winter holidays.

OpenAI's response: "Training chat models is not a clean industrial process."

**Source**: [Is ChatGPT getting lazier over the holidays?](https://www.semafor.com/article/12/12/2023/is-chatgpt-getting-lazier-over-the-holidays) (Semafor, December 2023)

### 3.4 Human Perception Bias and Novelty Decay

When a model is new, users are impressed. Over time:

- Users discover edge cases and limitations
- The "wow factor" fades, making the same performance feel worse
- Users develop more complex use cases that stress the model's weaknesses
- Confirmation bias: once you believe the model is worse, you notice every mistake

This is a real and significant effect, but it's NOT the whole story — as the Stanford
paper and Anthropic postmortem prove, real degradation also happens.

### 3.5 Goodhart's Law and Benchmark Gaming

"When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure."

Model providers optimize for benchmarks, which means:
- Models score higher on standardized tests but feel less useful for everyday tasks
- Companies privately test many model versions and publish only the best results
- Even modest access to evaluation data can boost scores by 112%
- The result: benchmark scores go UP while user satisfaction goes DOWN

GPT-5.4 scores higher than GPT-4 on benchmarks, yet users perceive it as worse for
day-to-day work. This is the "alignment tax" in action.

## Part 5: Implications for DumbDetector

### What to Track

1. **Subjective quality reports** — "Is the model dumb right now?" (per model, per provider)
2. **Token burn rate** — "Is the model wasteful?" (verbose, looping, unnecessary tool calls)
3. **Time-of-day patterns** — Correlate reports with likely peak load periods
4. **Cross-model correlation** — If multiple models degrade simultaneously, it's likely
   infrastructure; if one model degrades, it's likely a model-specific change
5. **Response characteristics** — Track average response length, refusal rate, code
   executability, language mixing (as canaries for infrastructure bugs)

### Key Insight for the Product

Users can't distinguish between these 13 mechanisms. They just know "it feels dumb." That's
exactly the gap DumbDetector fills — the user doesn't need to diagnose WHY, they just need
to know: "Is it just me, or is everyone experiencing this?"

The value proposition is validated by the fact that even Anthropic took weeks to discover
their own infrastructure bugs, and only because user reports reached critical mass. A
real-time crowdsourced signal would have caught these issues in hours, not weeks.
